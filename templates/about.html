The Economics of Common-Pool Resources

This game simulates a common-pool resource problem where multiple agents extract groundwater from a shared aquifer. The game illustrates fundamental concepts in environmental economics, including externalities, dynamic resource management, and the tragedy of the commons.

1. The Setting

Consider \(n\) farmers, each operating one well, who extract groundwater from a common aquifer. The aquifer has a stock level \(S_t\) at time \(t\), which evolves according to:

\[S_{t+1} = \max\left\{0, S_t + R - \sum_{i=1}^{n} q_{i,t}\right\}\]

where:

  • \(S_t\) = aquifer stock at the beginning of period \(t\)
  • \(R\) = natural recharge rate per period (constant)
  • \(q_{i,t}\) = extraction by farmer \(i\) in period \(t\)
  • \(n\) = number of farmers

2. The Profit Function

Each farmer's single-period profit depends on the price of water and a depth-dependent extraction cost that rises as the aquifer depletes. The profit function for farmer \(i\) in period \(t\) is:

\[\pi_{i,t}(q_{i,t}, S_t) = P \cdot q_{i,t} - \frac{\gamma}{2} q_{i,t}^2 - c (S_{\max} - S_t) q_{i,t}\]

where:

  • \(P\) = price per unit of water (revenue parameter)
  • \(\gamma\) = diminishing returns parameter
  • \(c\) = depth cost coefficient
  • \(S_{\max}\) = maximum aquifer capacity

The term \(c (S_{\max} - S_t) q_{i,t}\) represents the depth cost: as the stock \(S_t\) decreases, pumping costs increase because water must be lifted from greater depths. This is the source of the externality in the game.

3. Solving the Two-Period Game by Backward Induction

To understand the optimal extraction strategy, we solve a simplified two-period version of the game (\(t = T{-}1, T\)) by backward induction. This captures the essential dynamic tradeoff while remaining tractable.

It is helpful to define the net marginal value of the first unit of water:

\[m_t = P - c(S_{\max} - S_t)\]

When \(S_t\) is high (near \(S_{\max}\)), \(m_t\) is close to \(P\). As the stock falls, \(m_t\) decreases. In terms of \(m_t\), per-well profit simplifies to:

\[\pi_{i,t} = m_t \, q_{i,t} - \frac{\gamma}{2} q_{i,t}^2\]

The stock transition \(S_{t+1} = S_t + R - nq_t\) (in the symmetric case) implies that \(m_t\) evolves as:

\[m_{t+1} = m_t + c(R - nq_t)\]

If total extraction exceeds recharge, \(m\) falls (depth costs rise). If extraction is below recharge, \(m\) rises.

Period \(T\): The Terminal Round

In the final period, there is no future to worry about. Each farmer maximizes single-period profit taking the stock \(S_T\) (equivalently, \(m_T\)) as given:

\[\max_{q_T} \; m_T \, q_T - \frac{\gamma}{2} q_T^2\]

The first-order condition gives:

\[m_T - \gamma q_T = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad q_T^* = \frac{m_T}{\gamma}\]

Period \(T{-}1\): Accounting for the Future

Now consider a player who controls \(k\) wells (we will set \(k = n\) for the solo game and \(k = 1\) for the group game). The player maximizes the sum of period \(T{-}1\) and period \(T\) profits across their wells:

\[\max_{q_{T-1}} \; k\left[m_{T-1}\, q_{T-1} - \frac{\gamma}{2} q_{T-1}^2\right] + k\left[m_T\, q_T^*(m_T) - \frac{\gamma}{2}(q_T^*)^2\right]\]

The critical link is that \(q_{T-1}\) affects \(m_T\) through the stock transition. With \(k\) wells each extracting \(q_{T-1}\):

\[m_T = m_{T-1} + c(R - nq_{T-1})\]

where total extraction includes all \(n\) wells (not just the \(k\) the player controls). The player's \(k\) wells contribute \(kq_{T-1}\) to this total.

Define the optimized period-\(T\) per-well profit as \(\Pi_T^*(m_T) = m_T q_T^* - \frac{\gamma}{2}(q_T^*)^2\). Since \(k\) factors out, the first-order condition with respect to \(q_{T-1}\) is:

\[m_{T-1} - \gamma q_{T-1} + \frac{d\Pi_T^*}{dm_T} \cdot \frac{\partial m_T}{\partial q_{T-1}} = 0\]

We evaluate each factor in turn. First, from the stock transition, the player's per-well extraction affects \(m_T\) through their \(k\) wells:

\[\frac{\partial m_T}{\partial q_{T-1}} = -kc\]

Second, we need the derivative of optimized period-\(T\) profit with respect to \(m_T\). Expanding with the product rule:

\[\frac{d\Pi_T^*}{dm_T} = q_T^* + (m_T - \gamma q_T^*)\frac{dq_T^*}{dm_T}\]

By the envelope theorem, the period-\(T\) first-order condition tells us \(m_T - \gamma q_T^* = 0\), so the second term vanishes:

\[\frac{d\Pi_T^*}{dm_T} = q_T^*\]

Combining:

\[m_{T-1} - \gamma q_{T-1} + q_T^* \cdot (-kc) = 0\]
\[q_{T-1}^* = \frac{m_{T-1}}{\gamma} - \frac{kc}{\gamma}\, q_T^*\]

This says: extract at the myopic (single-period) rate \(m_{T-1}/\gamma\), minus a correction term \(\frac{kc}{\gamma}\, q_T^*\) that accounts for the future cost of lowering the stock across the \(k\) wells the player controls.

4. Solo Game vs. Group Game

Solo Game (\(k = n\))

In solo mode, the player controls all \(n\) wells. Setting \(k = n\):

\[q_{T-1}^{\text{solo}} = \frac{m_{T-1}}{\gamma} - \frac{nc}{\gamma}\, q_T^*\]

The correction term is \(\frac{nc}{\gamma}\, q_T^*\). It is large because the player internalizes the full social cost: each unit of per-well extraction lowers \(S\) by \(n\) (applied across all \(n\) wells), and the resulting cost increase hits all \(n\) of the player's own wells.

Group Game (\(k = 1\))

In the group game, each farmer controls a single well. Setting \(k = 1\):

\[q_{T-1}^{\text{Nash}} = \frac{m_{T-1}}{\gamma} - \frac{c}{\gamma}\, q_T^*\]

The correction term is only \(\frac{c}{\gamma}\, q_T^*\). The Nash farmer accounts for the effect of their extraction on their own single well's future costs, but ignores the cost imposed on the other \(n - 1\) farmers.

The Wedge

Key result: Comparing the two:
\[q_{T-1}^{\text{Nash}} - q_{T-1}^{\text{solo}} = \frac{(n-1)\,c}{\gamma}\, q_T^*\]
The Nash farmer extracts more than the solo player by exactly \(\frac{(n-1)\,c}{\gamma}\, q_T^*\) per well. This is the uninternalized externality — the future cost each farmer imposes on the other \(n - 1\) farmers by lowering the stock. Because the Nash farmer ignores this, they overextract relative to the social optimum.

Equivalently, you can view this from the conservation side: if a Nash farmer restrains their extraction by one unit, \(S_T\) rises by one unit, but the benefit of that higher stock is shared across all \(n\) farmers. The farmer bears the full cost of restraint but captures only \(1/n\) of the benefit. Conservation is a bad investment when others free-ride on it — so everyone overextracts.

5. Types of Externalities

Stock Externality

Definition: A stock externality occurs when one agent's extraction today reduces the resource stock available in future periods, imposing costs on all users (including oneself) through higher future extraction costs.

This is the externality at the heart of the game. When farmer \(i\) extracts \(q_{i,t}\), the aquifer stock decreases to \(S_{t+1} = S_t + R - \sum_j q_{j,t}\). In the next period, everyone faces higher pumping costs because \(S_{t+1} < S_t\) (assuming total extraction exceeds recharge).

The marginal stock externality is:

\[\frac{\partial \pi_{i,t+1}}{\partial q_{j,t}} = c \cdot q_{i,t+1}\]

This shows that an additional unit extracted by farmer \(j\) today increases farmer \(i\)'s costs tomorrow by \(c \cdot q_{i,t+1}\).

Congestion Externality

Definition: A congestion externality arises when agents' simultaneous use of a resource within the same period creates interference, reducing the effectiveness or increasing the cost of each agent's use.

Our model does not include a congestion externality. The \(-\frac{\gamma}{2} q_{i,t}^2\) term in the profit function depends only on farmer \(i\)'s own extraction — it is a private cost reflecting diminishing returns to pumping effort at an individual well, not interference between farmers.

To incorporate a true congestion externality, we would need extraction costs that depend on aggregate extraction within a period. For example, we could replace the private quadratic cost with a term like:

\[-\frac{\gamma}{2} \left(\sum_{j=1}^{n} q_{j,t}\right) q_{i,t}\]

Under this formulation, farmer \(i\)'s cost would increase when other farmers pump more in the same period, capturing phenomena such as pump interference or local drawdown effects when multiple wells operate simultaneously. This would create a within-period strategic interaction on top of the across-period stock externality. The game focuses on the stock externality alone to isolate its effects clearly.

6. Social Optimum vs. Nash Equilibrium

A social planner maximizing total welfare across all \(n\) farmers would choose extraction levels to maximize the sum of aggregate profits, accounting for the dynamic evolution of the stock.

In a finite-horizon setting with \(T\) periods, the planner solves:

\[\max_{\{q_{i,t}\}} \; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \pi_{i,t}(q_{i,t}, S_t)\]

subject to:

\[S_{t+1} = \max\{0, S_t + R - \sum_{i=1}^{n} q_{i,t}\}, \quad S_1 = S_0\]

The planner's first-order condition for \(q_{i,t}\) includes an additional term representing the shadow value of the stock:

\[P - \gamma q_{i,t} - c(S_{\max} - S_t) = \lambda_t\]

where \(\lambda_t\) is the marginal user cost (the present value of future cost increases from reducing \(S_t\) by one unit).

In the Nash equilibrium, farmers set \(\lambda_t \approx 0\) because they internalize only a small fraction (\(1/n^2\)) of the true social cost of their extraction. This results in:

  • Overextraction: \(Q^{\text{Nash}} > Q^{\text{Optimal}}\)
  • Faster stock depletion: The aquifer depletes more rapidly under Nash behavior
  • Lower aggregate welfare: Total profits are lower than under cooperative management

7. Comparative Statics

Using the myopic Nash equilibrium extraction level \(q^* = \frac{P - c (S_{\max} - S)}{\gamma} = \frac{m}{\gamma}\), we can analyze how equilibrium behavior changes with parameters:

Effect of Stock Level

\[\frac{\partial q^*}{\partial S} = \frac{c}{\gamma} > 0\]

Lower stock \(\Rightarrow\) higher costs \(\Rightarrow\) lower extraction. As the aquifer depletes, it becomes less profitable to pump, naturally slowing extraction (though not preventing eventual collapse if \(R < n \cdot q^*\)).

Effect of Number of Farmers

Total extraction \(Q^* = n \cdot q^*\) increases linearly with \(n\). More users intensify the tragedy of the commons.

Effect of Recharge Rate

While \(R\) doesn't directly affect \(q^*\), higher recharge sustains the stock at higher levels over time, indirectly supporting higher extraction rates in equilibrium.
