The Economics of Common-Pool Resources

This game simulates a common-pool resource problem where multiple agents extract groundwater from a shared aquifer. The game illustrates fundamental concepts in environmental economics, including externalities, dynamic resource management, and the tragedy of the commons.

1. The Setting

Consider \(n\) farmers, each operating one well, who extract groundwater from a common aquifer. The aquifer has a stock level \(S_t\) at time \(t\), which evolves according to:

\[S_{t+1} = \max\left\{0, S_t + R - \sum_{i=1}^{n} q_{i,t}\right\}\]

where:

  • \(S_t\) = aquifer stock at the beginning of period \(t\)
  • \(R\) = natural recharge rate per period (constant)
  • \(q_{i,t}\) = extraction by farmer \(i\) in period \(t\)
  • \(n\) = number of farmers

2. The Profit Function

Each farmer's single-period profit depends on the price of water, extraction costs, and a congestion cost from pumping. The profit function for farmer \(i\) in period \(t\) is:

\[\pi_{i,t}(q_{i,t}, S_t) = P \cdot q_{i,t} - \frac{\gamma}{2} q_{i,t}^2 - c_0 \cdot q_{i,t} - c_1 (S_{\max} - S_t) q_{i,t}\]

where:

  • \(P\) = price per unit of water (revenue parameter)
  • \(\gamma\) = congestion parameter (captures diminishing returns to extraction)
  • \(c_0\) = base extraction cost per unit
  • \(c_1\) = depth cost coefficient
  • \(S_{\max}\) = maximum aquifer capacity

The term \(c_1 (S_{\max} - S_t) q_{i,t}\) represents the stock externality: as the stock \(S_t\) decreases, pumping costs increase because water must be lifted from greater depths.

3. Types of Externalities

Stock Externality

Definition: A stock externality occurs when one agent's extraction today reduces the resource stock available in future periods, imposing costs on all users (including oneself) through higher future extraction costs.

In this game, when farmer \(i\) extracts \(q_{i,t}\), the aquifer stock decreases to \(S_{t+1} = S_t + R - \sum_j q_{j,t}\). In the next period, everyone faces higher pumping costs because \(S_{t+1} < S_t\) (assuming total extraction exceeds recharge).

The marginal stock externality is:

\[\frac{\partial \pi_{i,t+1}}{\partial q_{j,t}} = c_1 \cdot q_{i,t+1}\]

This shows that an additional unit extracted by farmer \(j\) today increases farmer \(i\)'s costs tomorrow by \(c_1 q_{i,t+1}\).

Congestion Externality

Definition: A congestion externality arises when agents' simultaneous use of a resource within the same period creates interference, reducing the effectiveness or increasing the cost of each agent's use.

The term \(-\frac{\gamma}{2} q_{i,t}^2\) in the profit function represents within-period congestion. Higher extraction \(q_{i,t}\) by farmer \(i\) results in quadratically increasing costs, capturing phenomena such as:

  • Pump interference when multiple wells operate simultaneously
  • Declining marginal productivity of pumping effort
  • Local drawdown effects around individual wells

While this is primarily a private cost in our formulation, in more complex models, congestion can also represent strategic externalities where one farmer's extraction directly affects others' extraction costs within the same period.

4. Nash Equilibrium in the Static Game

Consider a single-period game where \(n\) farmers simultaneously choose extraction levels, taking the stock \(S\) as given. Each farmer \(i\) solves:

\[\max_{q_i} \; \pi_i(q_i, S) = P \cdot q_i - \frac{\gamma}{2} q_i^2 - c_0 \cdot q_i - c_1 (S_{\max} - S) q_i\]

The first-order condition is:

\[\frac{\partial \pi_i}{\partial q_i} = P - \gamma q_i - c_0 - c_1 (S_{\max} - S) = 0\]

Solving for \(q_i\):

\[q_i^* = \frac{P - c_0 - c_1 (S_{\max} - S)}{\gamma}\]

Since all farmers are identical, in a symmetric Nash equilibrium, each chooses the same quantity \(q^*\). The total extraction is:

\[Q^* = n \cdot q^* = \frac{n[P - c_0 - c_1 (S_{\max} - S)]}{\gamma}\]

Key insight: Individual farmers do not internalize the stock externality. They ignore the fact that their extraction today increases costs for everyone (including themselves) tomorrow. This leads to overextraction relative to the socially optimal level.

5. Social Optimum vs. Nash Equilibrium

A social planner maximizing total welfare across all \(n\) farmers would choose extraction levels to maximize the present discounted value of aggregate profits, accounting for the dynamic evolution of the stock.

In a finite-horizon setting with \(T\) periods and discount factor \(\delta\), the planner solves:

\[\max_{\{q_{i,t}\}} \; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \delta^{t-1} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \pi_{i,t}(q_{i,t}, S_t)\]

subject to:

\[S_{t+1} = \max\{0, S_t + R - \sum_{i=1}^{n} q_{i,t}\}, \quad S_1 = S_0\]

The planner's first-order condition for \(q_{i,t}\) includes an additional term representing the shadow value of the stock:

\[P - \gamma q_{i,t} - c_0 - c_1(S_{\max} - S_t) = \lambda_t\]

where \(\lambda_t\) is the marginal user cost (the present value of future cost increases from reducing \(S_t\) by one unit).

In the Nash equilibrium, farmers set \(\lambda_t = 0\) because they do not account for future impacts. This results in:

  • Overextraction: \(Q^{\text{Nash}} > Q^{\text{Optimal}}\)
  • Faster stock depletion: The aquifer depletes more rapidly under Nash behavior
  • Lower aggregate welfare: Total profits are lower than under cooperative management

6. Comparative Statics

Using the Nash equilibrium extraction level \(q^* = \frac{P - c_0 - c_1 (S_{\max} - S)}{\gamma}\), we can analyze how equilibrium behavior changes with parameters:

Effect of Stock Level

\[\frac{\partial q^*}{\partial S} = \frac{c_1}{\gamma} > 0\]

Lower stock \(\Rightarrow\) higher costs \(\Rightarrow\) lower extraction. As the aquifer depletes, it becomes less profitable to pump, naturally slowing extraction (though not preventing eventual collapse if \(R < n \cdot q^*\)).

Effect of Number of Farmers

Total extraction \(Q^* = n \cdot q^*\) increases linearly with \(n\). More users intensify the tragedy of the commons.

Effect of Recharge Rate

While \(R\) doesn't directly affect \(q^*\), higher recharge sustains the stock at higher levels over time, indirectly supporting higher extraction rates in equilibrium.

7. Policy Implications

The gap between Nash equilibrium and social optimum suggests several policy interventions:

  • Pigouvian taxes: A tax equal to the marginal external cost \(\lambda_t\) would align private and social incentives.
  • Quotas: Limiting each farmer to \(q_i \leq \bar{q}\) where \(\bar{q} < q^*\) can prevent overextraction.
  • Tradable permits: Allocating \(n \cdot \bar{q}\) total extraction rights and allowing trade can achieve efficiency while respecting equity concerns.
  • Cooperative management: If farmers can credibly commit to coordinated extraction, they can achieve the social optimum.

This game allows students to experience firsthand why such policies are necessary: even rational, profit-maximizing individuals will collectively deplete a shared resource without institutional constraints.

8. Extensions and Further Reading

This model can be extended in several directions:

  • Heterogeneous agents: Farmers with different cost structures or discount rates
  • Stochastic recharge: \(R_t\) varies randomly, introducing uncertainty
  • Strategic behavior: Repeated game dynamics with punishment strategies
  • Spatial externalities: Location-dependent pumping costs and stock interactions
