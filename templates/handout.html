Groundwater Commons Game

Student Handout  |  Environmental Economics

Overview

You are a farmer who pumps groundwater to irrigate your crops. The water comes from a shared underground aquifer. You will make pumping decisions across several rounds and try to maximize your total profit — but so will everyone else sharing your aquifer. Your choices affect the water level that everyone faces in future rounds.

Big idea: This game illustrates the tragedy of the commons — a situation where individually rational decisions lead to a collectively worse outcome for a shared resource.

How the Aquifer Works

The aquifer has a stock level (S) that changes every round based on how much water everyone pumps and how much naturally recharges:

Snext = max(0,   Scurrent + R − total pumping)
  • S = current water stock in the aquifer
  • R = natural recharge per round (constant)
  • Total pumping = sum of all players' extractions this round

If the group pumps more than the aquifer recharges, the stock declines. If the stock reaches zero, pumping becomes very expensive and profits collapse. The game ends after 8 rounds (or when the aquifer is exhausted).

How Your Profit Is Calculated

Each round, your profit depends on how much you pump (q) and the current water level (S):

Profit = P·q − (γ/2)·q² − c0·q − c1·(Smax − S)·q
Term What it represents Default value
P · q Revenue from selling your water P = 10
(γ/2) · q² Congestion cost — pumping too much at once has diminishing returns γ = 0.08
c0 · q Base operating cost per unit pumped c0 = 2
c1 · (Smax − S) · q Depth cost — the lower the water table, the more expensive to pump c1 = 0.006
Key insight: When the aquifer depletes, pumping costs rise for everyone. This is the stock externality — your pumping today imposes costs on all players (including yourself) in future rounds.

Game Modes

Solo Mode

You control all 6 wells by yourself. Set the same pumping rate for each well and observe how the aquifer responds over 8 rounds. Use this to explore the dynamics before playing with your group.

Good questions to explore: What happens if you pump at full capacity every round? What if you pump conservatively?

Group Mode

Each student controls one well. You all share the same aquifer. Every round you independently choose your pumping rate, and the round advances once everyone submits. The player with the highest cumulative profit at the end wins.

How to Play — Group Mode

1Navigate to the game website and click Group Mode.
2One person in your group acts as host: click "Host Controls," set the number of players, then click Create Room. Share the 5-character room code with your group.
3All other players click "Join as Player," enter the room code and your name, then click Join Room.
4Once everyone has joined, the host clicks Start Game.
5Each round: move the slider to set your pumping rate (0 to qmax), then click Finalize Pumping Decision. The round advances automatically when all players have submitted.
6After 8 rounds, a final leaderboard shows cumulative profits for all players.

What to Think About

  • What pumping rate maximizes your profit in a single round, ignoring future costs?
  • What pumping rate would be best for the group as a whole over all 8 rounds?
  • Why might these two answers differ? What happens to the aquifer if everyone pumps to maximize their individual round profit?
  • Did your group implicitly cooperate, or did you experience overextraction? How did the aquifer stock change over time?
  • What real-world policies or institutions could help groups avoid the tragedy of the commons?

Key Terms

TermDefinition
Stock externalityEach unit you extract today lowers the aquifer level, raising pumping costs for everyone in every future round.
Congestion externalityPumping a large quantity within a single round faces increasing per-unit costs (captured by the γ term).
Nash equilibriumThe outcome where each player is choosing their best response to everyone else's choices — often leads to overextraction here.
Social optimumThe pumping plan that maximizes total group profit across all rounds, accounting for future stock effects.
Tragedy of the commonsWhen individually rational behavior leads to collective overuse and degradation of a shared resource.
